## Why Supermarket Basil Dies and How to Save It

The basil plant you buy at the grocery store — a dense cluster of 20-30 seedlings in a 4-inch pot — is designed to die. It was grown in a greenhouse at 75°F with 16 hours of supplemental light, packed into a plastic sleeve, shipped in a refrigerated truck (basil is cold-sensitive — temperatures below 50°F cause cellular damage), and placed on a shelf where it competes with 29 other seedlings for the water and nutrients in 2 cups of potting mix.

**The fix**: Immediately divide the clump into individual plants. Gently pull apart the root-bound mass and transplant the strongest 4-6 seedlings into separate 4-6 inch pots with quality potting mix. Discard the weak runts. Each survivor gets its own root space. Water thoroughly and place in the brightest window you have. The plant will look shocked for 3-5 days — this is transplant stress, not death. It recovers and thrives.

## Light: The Non-Negotiable Requirement

Basil is a full-sun Mediterranean herb. It requires a minimum of 6 hours of direct sunlight or 12-14 hours of artificial light daily. A south-facing windowsill (Northern Hemisphere) is adequate from April through September. From October through March, the sun is too low and daylight hours too short — even a south-facing window needs supplemental light.

### Light Schedule

12-14 hours on, 10-12 hours off. Use a mechanical outlet timer ($8-$12) — basil needs a consistent photoperiod. Irregular light cycles stress the plant. Do not run lights 24/7 — plants, like people, need a dark period for metabolic processes (respiration and starch mobilization occur primarily at night).

### How to Tell If Light Is Insufficient

- **Leggy growth**: Long gaps (3+ inches) between leaf pairs on the stem. The plant is stretching toward the light.
- **Pale, small leaves**: Insufficient light means insufficient photosynthesis — the plant produces fewer sugars, resulting in stunted, pale foliage.
- **Leaning toward the window**: Rotate the pot 90 degrees every 3-4 days to maintain symmetrical growth.
- **No new growth**: The plant survives but does not grow — it is in metabolic stasis.

## Soil, Water, and Fertilizer

### Soil
A fast-draining mix is essential. 50% quality potting mix + 30% perlite + 20% compost. Basil roots rot in heavy, water-retentive soil. A 6-8 inch pot with multiple drainage holes is large enough for one full-sized basil plant. Do not use a pot without drainage holes — basil is unforgiving of wet feet.

### Water
When the top 1 inch of soil is dry to the touch. In a 6-inch pot indoors, this is typically every 3-5 days. Water thoroughly until it runs from drainage holes, then dump the saucer after 30 minutes. Basil wilts dramatically when thirsty (leaves droop) and recovers within hours of watering — but repeated wilting stresses the plant and reduces oil production. Do not let it wilt as a watering reminder.

### Fertilizer
Basil grown indoors in containers needs regular feeding — the limited soil volume is exhausted of nutrients within 4-6 weeks.

- **Weeks 1-6**: The initial potting mix provides sufficient nutrients. No fertilizer.
- **Week 7+**: Liquid fish emulsion (5-1-1) or kelp fertilizer at half strength every 2 weeks. Alternatively, Osmocote controlled-release pellets applied once at week 6.
- **Signs of under-fertilization**: Pale green to yellow lower leaves, slow growth. **Signs of over-fertilization**: Dark green, lush leaves with diluted flavor (excess nitrogen reduces essential oil concentration).

## Harvesting for Continuous Production

The key to indoor basil that produces for 6-12 months (not 6-8 weeks) is harvesting correctly:

1. **Never harvest individual leaves**: Pinch or cut the stem just above a pair of leaves. The plant responds by producing two new stems from the node below the cut — doubling your future harvest points.
2. **Harvest from the top down**: The top 2-3 leaf pairs are the most tender and flavorful. Cutting the growing tip also prevents flowering (see below).
3. **Never harvest more than 1/3 of the plant at once**: The plant needs remaining foliage for photosynthesis. Stripping a plant bare (e.g., to make one batch of pesto) kills it or sets it back 4-6 weeks.
4. **Harvest regularly — at least weekly**: Regular harvesting is the growth stimulus. A basil plant that is never harvested bolts (flowers) and declines within 2-3 months. A regularly harvested basil plant remains in vegetative (leaf-producing) mode for 12+ months.

## Preventing Flowering

Once basil flowers, leaf production drops by 70-90% as the plant redirects energy to seed production. The leaves that remain are smaller, tougher, and less flavorful. Indoor basil under grow lights is especially prone to flowering because the consistent 12-14 hour photoperiod mimics the long days of summer that trigger the flowering response.

**To prevent flowering**: Pinch off the central flower bud and the two buds immediately below it the moment they appear. Check every 3-4 days — basil flowers develop fast. A regularly harvested plant rarely flowers because you are constantly removing the growing tips where flower buds form.

If the plant has already flowered: cut all stems back by 50%, remove every flower and flower bud, give it a dose of liquid fertilizer, and increase light by 2 hours per day for 2 weeks. The plant often reverts to vegetative growth — but the recovered leaves are never quite as flavorful as pre-flowering growth.

## Propagation: Infinite Free Basil

The supermarket basil trick (dividing seedlings) works once. For a perpetual supply, propagate from cuttings:

1. Cut a 4-6 inch stem just below a leaf node (the bump where leaves attach).
2. Remove the lower 2-3 pairs of leaves — these would rot underwater.
3. Place the cutting in a glass of water on a bright windowsill. Change water every 2-3 days.
4. Roots emerge within 7-10 days. Wait until roots are 2-3 inches long.
5. Transplant into a 4-inch pot with moist potting mix. Keep out of direct light for 3-4 days while the cutting acclimates.
6. Success rate: ~90% with Genovese basil. One healthy mother plant provides 10-20 cuttings per year.

## Key Takeaways

Basil needs 12-14 hours of bright light daily — a south window is adequate April-September, but a $25 LED shop light is required October-March. Divide supermarket basil into individual plants immediately. Pinch stems above leaf pairs for harvesting — never strip individual leaves. Remove flower buds the instant they appear. Propagate from cuttings for infinite free basil. With proper light and harvesting, a single basil plant produces continuously for 12+ months indoors.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I grow basil indoors without a grow light?

Yes — from April through September in a south-facing window. From October through March in northern latitudes (Zones 3-7), even a south window does not provide enough light intensity or duration. The plant survives but produces spindly, pale growth with low oil concentration. A $25 LED shop light hung 6-8 inches above the plant for 12-14 hours daily provides sufficient light year-round.

### Why is my indoor basil turning yellow?

Overwatering is the most common cause. Basil likes consistently moist but not wet soil. Let the top 1 inch dry between waterings. Second cause: nitrogen deficiency — pale yellow older leaves. Solution: liquid fish emulsion at half strength. Third cause: cold temperatures — basil leaves yellow below 55°F. Move the plant away from drafty windows in winter.

### How long does an indoor basil plant live?

12-18 months with proper care. Basil is technically a short-lived perennial in its native tropical habitat but is grown as an annual in temperate climates. Indoors, with consistent warmth (65-80°F) and light, it produces for 12-15 months before declining. Take cuttings at month 10-12 to start the next generation — do not wait for the mother plant to die.

### Can I grow basil from seed indoors?

Yes — this is often more successful than supermarket plants. Sow seeds 1/4 inch deep in moist seed-starting mix. Germination: 5-10 days at 70-75°F. Thin to the strongest seedling. Harvest begins at 6-8 weeks. The advantage over supermarket plants: no transplant shock, no overcrowding, and you can choose compact varieties (''Spicy Globe'', ''Boxwood'') bred for containers.

### Why does my basil taste bland?

Insufficient light is the #1 cause. Essential oil concentration is directly proportional to light intensity. Indoor basil under weak light produces large, fast-growing leaves with low oil content — they look impressive but taste like nothing. Increase light (stronger bulb, closer to plant, or longer duration). Second cause: over-fertilization with high-nitrogen fertilizer produces lush growth with diluted flavor.
